Your Buyer's Power Move

The Notice to Seller to Perform (NSP)

~30 min RPA Paragraph 14D / 14E / C.A.R. Form NSP 5 Questions
📖 THE STORY CONTINUES

It is Day 9. The Okonkwos are on track but something is wrong on the seller's side. The seller was supposed to deliver all disclosures by Day 7. Nothing has arrived — no TDS, no SPQ, no NHD.

You have called the listing agent twice. You sent an email. All you got back was: 'We are working on it.'

Your buyers' contingency deadlines are still counting down. It is time to flip the script. Your buyers have a power move too.

What Is the Notice to Seller to Perform?

The Notice to Seller to Perform (C.A.R. Form NSP) is the buyer's equivalent of the NBP. It is a formal written notice from the buyer: "You missed your obligation. You have 2 days to perform — or I have the right to cancel and get my deposit back."

Most new agents only learn about the NBP. But the NSP is equally important — it is how your buyer protects themselves when the seller drops the ball.

RPA Paragraph 14D(2) — Buyer Right to Cancel; Seller Contract Obligations

If the seller has not performed any contractual obligation by the time specified — including delivering required documents and disclosures — the buyer, after first delivering an NSP, may cancel and the seller shall authorize return of the buyer's deposit.

When Can Your Buyer Send an NSP?

1
Seller has not delivered required disclosures by the deadline

TDS, SPQ, NHD and all required disclosures must arrive within 7 days (Paragraph N(1) default). Miss it — NSP.

2
Seller has not ordered or delivered HOA documents

HOA docs must be ordered within 3 days of acceptance. Delay triggers the buyer's NSP right.

3
Seller has not completed agreed-upon repairs before close

If repairs were part of the negotiated agreement and seller has not completed them, NSP applies.

4
Seller has not removed their own contingency by the deadline

If the seller had a contingency and has not removed it on time, buyer can send NSP.

The Rules — Same as the NBP

The NSP follows the same rules as the NBP — must be in writing, signed by the buyer (not just the agent), and gives the seller at least 2 days after delivery to perform. Cannot be delivered more than 2 days before the seller's scheduled performance deadline.

An Incorrectly Delivered NSP Is Void

If the NSP is delivered too early, not signed by the buyer, or gives the seller less time than required — it is invalid and void. The buyer must deliver a corrected NSP. (RPA Paragraph 14E)

The Strategic Power of the NSP

In our scenario, the seller missed the Day 7 disclosure deadline. On Day 9, you prepare an NSP, get it signed by Marcus and Priya, and deliver it. This gives the seller 2 days to deliver the required disclosures.

If the seller performs within the NSP window — the transaction continues normally. If they still do not perform — your buyers can cancel and recover their full deposit.

Here is the strategic power: most of the time, it works. When a seller sees a formal notice with a 2-day deadline and a cancellation threat, the disclosures appear very quickly.

Do Not Wait Too Long to Send the NSP

The NSP is most powerful when used promptly after a seller misses a deadline. Waiting creates ambiguity about whether you waived the deadline through inaction. Track seller obligations just as carefully as buyer obligations — the timelines run in both directions.

Stay Within Your License

You can prepare the NSP for your buyer's signature and deliver it to the seller's agent. You cannot determine whether a seller's performance was legally sufficient or advise on whether a breach rises to a remedy beyond cancellation. For complex situations, refer your buyer to a California real estate attorney.
