Your Buyer's Power Move

The Notice to Seller to Perform (NSP) — When and How to Use It
~30 min readRPA ¶14D, ¶14E5 Quiz Questions
📋 Day 8 — The Seller Goes Silent

It's Day 8. The seller's disclosures were due by Day 7. Nothing has arrived. No TDS, no SPQ, no NHD. You call the listing agent — no response. Your buyers are getting nervous.

You have a tool for this. It's the NSP — the buyer's version of the NBP.

What Is the NSP?

The Notice to Seller to Perform (C.A.R. Form NSP) is the BUYER's formal notice that the SELLER has failed to meet a contractual obligation. It is the mirror image of the NBP — same rules, same requirements, opposite party. It is a required prerequisite before the buyer can cancel based on seller non-performance.

RPA Reference: ¶14D — Buyer's right to cancel for seller non-performance, which requires first delivering an NSP. ¶14E — Same requirements apply to both NBP and NSP.

When to Send an NSP

Common triggers for an NSP include:

  • Seller failed to deliver disclosures (TDS, SPQ, NHD) by the 7-day deadline
  • Seller failed to deliver HOA documents on time
  • Seller failed to make agreed-upon repairs
  • Any seller obligation not met by its deadline

NSP Requirements — Same as NBP

Per ¶14E:

  1. Must be in writing
  2. Must be signed by the buyer
  3. Must give the seller at least 2 days to perform
📌 After the NSP

If the seller delivers the missing items within 2 days — problem solved, transaction continues. If they don't, the buyer may cancel and receive their full deposit back.
