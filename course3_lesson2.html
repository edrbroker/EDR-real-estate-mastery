The Disclosure Package

TDS, SPQ, NHD, AVID — What Each Form Does and Why It Matters
~30 min readRPA ¶11A, ¶11B5 Quiz Questions
📋 Day 3 — The Disclosure Meeting

You\'re on Zoom with David and Sarah Chen. You have the TDS pulled up on your screen. David is nervous — he keeps asking, "Do we have to disclose the roof thing? It was fixed two years ago."

Yes. You do. You walk them through every section of the TDS, the SPQ, and explain why the NHD has already been ordered. By the end of the call, they understand that full disclosure isn\'t just a legal requirement — it\'s what protects them from a lawsuit three years after close.

The Transfer Disclosure Statement (TDS)

The TDS is the cornerstone of California seller disclosure law. Required by Civil Code §1102 for all 1–4 unit residential sales (with limited exemptions), it is a standardized form divided into sections: the seller\'s section, the listing agent\'s section, and the buyer\'s acknowledgment.

The TDS asks the seller to disclose known conditions affecting the property — systems, appliances, structural issues, neighborhood conditions, and legal matters. "Known" is the key word: sellers are not required to discover defects, but they cannot conceal ones they know about.

📌 What "Fully Completed" Means

Under ¶11A(2), the TDS is only considered "fully completed" when: (i) the seller has checked all boxes in Section I, (ii) the seller has answered all questions and signed Section II, and (iii) the listing agent has completed and signed Section III (or an AVID has been completed instead).

Seller Property Questionnaire (SPQ)

The SPQ supplements the TDS with more detailed questions about the property's history. While the TDS focuses on current known conditions, the SPQ digs deeper — insurance claims, permits, water intrusion history, neighborhood nuisances, HOA issues, and more. Under ¶11A(3), the seller completes and signs the SPQ and delivers it to the buyer within the 7-day window.

RPA Reference: ¶11A(3) — If the seller is exempt from providing a TDS, they must still provide either an SPQ or an Exempt Seller Disclosure (ESD). There is no disclosure-free transaction.

Natural Hazard Disclosure (NHD)

The NHD is a statutory disclosure identifying whether the property is in any of six state-designated natural hazard zones: Special Flood Hazard Area, Area of Potential Flooding, High Fire Hazard Severity Zone, Wildland Fire Area, Earthquake Fault Zone, or Seismic Hazard Zone. The seller is required to deliver the NHD, but in practice, listing agents order it from a third-party NHD company on the seller\'s behalf.

5555 Winners Lane is in Los Angeles — agents should check whether the property falls in a high fire hazard zone, which would also trigger the Fire Hardening Disclosure (FHDS) requirement under ¶11C.

Agent Visual Inspection Disclosure (AVID)

The AVID documents what the listing agent visually observed during their inspection of the property. It is the agent\'s disclosure — not the seller\'s — and it protects the agent by creating a written record of what was observed and disclosed. The AVID can substitute for the agent\'s section of the TDS (Section III).

✓ Pro Tip

Complete the AVID every time you take a listing, even if nothing appears wrong. It documents that you looked. Agents who skip the AVID on "clean" properties end up in disputes later.

Amended Disclosures — The Ongoing Obligation

Disclosure isn\'t a one-time event. Under ¶11A(4), if the seller OR their agent becomes aware of a material adverse condition after delivering the TDS — right up until close of escrow — they must promptly deliver an amended TDS or SPQ. This is critical: the obligation to disclose continues throughout the transaction.

🚨 Watch For This

A seller who discovers a leak, receives a notice from the HOA, or gets a contractor's report about a defect during escrow must update their disclosures immediately. Failure to amend is grounds for a lawsuit post-close.
