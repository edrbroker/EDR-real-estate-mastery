The 5 Core Buyer Contingencies

What Each One Protects — And What Happens When They Expire

~30 min read RPA Paragraph 8 / Section L 5 Quiz Questions

It's Day 3. Marcus just confirmed the EMD wire hit escrow. Priya texted you a photo of paint swatches she's already picked out for the living room at 5555 Winners Lane.

You smile — but you also know that right now, your buyers have 5 powerful legal protections built into their contract. These are their contingencies. Each one gives them the right to cancel and get their deposit back if something specific goes wrong.

Your job is to understand every single one — what it covers, when it expires, and what it means to remove it. Because the moment those protections disappear, your buyer's deposit is on the line.

What Is a Contingency?

A contingency is a condition that must be met for the sale to move forward. If the condition isn't met, the buyer has the right to cancel the contract and get their deposit back — as long as the contingency is still active.

Think of each contingency like a safety net under your buyer. As long as it's in place, they can walk away. The moment it's removed in writing — that net disappears for that specific issue.

⚠️ Critical Rule — RPA Paragraph 8I

A buyer has no obligation to remove a contingency until the seller has delivered all required documents, reports, and information related to that contingency. Removing a contingency before receiving all required information means relinquishing those contractual rights. The RPA states this is done against agent advice.

The 5 Contingencies Protecting Marcus & Priya

1
Loan Contingency
¶8A / L(1)  ·  Default: 17 days after acceptance
🛡️ What It Protects

If Marcus and Priya cannot obtain the loan they applied for — even after acting diligently and in good faith — they can cancel and get their deposit back.

📍 5555 Winners Lane — Real Scenario

The Okonkwos applied for a $1,000,000 loan. On Day 14, their lender calls and says the loan was denied due to a recent job change Marcus hadn't disclosed. The loan contingency is still active. They can cancel and recover their deposit.

⚠️ Agent Must Know

If the deal falls apart solely because the property didn't appraise AND there is no appraisal contingency, the buyer cannot use the loan contingency as an escape. The loan and appraisal contingencies are separate. Removing one does not remove the other. (RPA ¶8A(4))

2
Appraisal Contingency
¶8B / L(2)  ·  Default: 17 days after acceptance
🛡️ What It Protects

If the property is appraised by a licensed appraiser at less than the purchase price (or the amount specified in the RPA), the buyer can cancel and get their deposit back.

📍 5555 Winners Lane — Real Scenario

Marcus and Priya offered $1,250,000. The appraisal comes in at $1,190,000 — $60,000 short. With the appraisal contingency in place, they can cancel, renegotiate the price, or make up the difference. Without it, they'd need to cover that $60,000 gap out of pocket or lose their deposit.

⚠️ Agent Must Know

If a buyer removes the appraisal contingency during a competitive offer situation, they are agreeing to buy the property regardless of what it appraises for. This is a significant financial risk your buyer must fully understand before signing. Always advise in writing.

3
Investigation of Property (Inspection) Contingency
¶8C / L(3)  ·  Default: 17 days after acceptance
🛡️ What It Protects

This is the broadest contingency. It protects the buyer's right to physically investigate the property — including hiring inspectors for the roof, foundation, pest, plumbing, electrical, and any other physical condition — and to cancel if they are not satisfied.

📍 5555 Winners Lane — Real Scenario

The home inspector finds significant foundation cracking and evidence of unpermitted work on the back bedroom. Priya is upset — this is a deal breaker. Because they're on Day 12 and the contingency is still active, they can cancel and get their full deposit back. Or they can use the Request for Repair form (C.A.R. Form RR) to ask the seller to fix it.

⚠️ Agent Must Know

Informational access to the property is NOT a contingency by itself (RPA ¶L(3)). Your buyer always has the right to access the property for information even after contingencies are removed — but they lose the right to cancel based on what they find once the investigation contingency is removed.

4
Insurance Contingency
¶8D / L(4)  ·  Default: 17 days after acceptance
🛡️ What It Protects

If the buyer cannot obtain homeowners insurance at an acceptable cost — or cannot get insured at all — they can cancel and get their deposit back. This includes fire insurance, flood insurance, and any policy the buyer needs or desires.

📍 5555 Winners Lane — Real Scenario

5555 Winners Lane is in a high fire hazard severity zone. Every insurance carrier Priya contacts either declines to cover the property or quotes $18,000/year — far more than the Okonkwos budgeted. The insurance contingency protects them. They can cancel and recover their deposit before Day 17.

⚠️ Agent Must Know — This Is a Big One in California

With California's insurance crisis, this contingency is more important than ever. The California Department of Insurance (DOI) strongly advises buyers to start exploring insurance options as early as Day 1. Refer your buyers to a licensed insurance broker immediately — do not wait. If they can't get insured, a failed loan contingency cannot be used as the reason to cancel if the real issue was insurance. (RPA ¶8A(2))

5
Title / Preliminary Report Contingency
¶8F / L(6)  ·  Default: 17 days after acceptance or 5 days after delivery, whichever is later
🛡️ What It Protects

The buyer's right to review the Preliminary Title Report ("Prelim") and cancel if they find title defects, unexpected liens, encumbrances, or easements they are not willing to accept.

📍 5555 Winners Lane — Real Scenario

The Prelim comes back showing an unpaid IRS tax lien of $85,000 against the seller — attached to the property. The Okonkwos review it with their escrow officer and determine the seller cannot resolve it before close. With the title contingency active, they can cancel. Or they negotiate with the seller to pay it off through escrow as a condition of closing.

⚠️ Agent Must Know

Buyers should never remove the title contingency before reviewing the Prelim carefully. If a revised Prelim is issued after an original one was delivered, buyers have 5 additional days to review and cancel if the revision shows material deviations. (RPA ¶8F(2))

Bonus: Review of Seller Documents

Technically this is contingency L(5) under Paragraph 8E — and it's one many new agents overlook. It gives Marcus and Priya the right to review all documents the seller is required to deliver — TDS, SPQ, NHD, and more — and cancel if they don't approve what they see.

The deadline is 17 days after acceptance, OR 5 days after delivery of those documents — whichever is later. So if the seller delivers documents late, your buyer automatically gets more time. (Covered in Lesson 1.)

Quick Reference — All 5 at a Glance

Loan · ¶8A / L(1)
Can't get the loan → cancel & recover deposit
Appraisal · ¶8B / L(2)
Home appraises low → cancel or renegotiate
Inspection · ¶8C / L(3)
Physical condition unacceptable → cancel or request repairs
Insurance · ¶8D / L(4)
Can't get insured at reasonable cost → cancel
Title · ¶8F / L(6)
Title defects, liens, or encumbrances → cancel or negotiate
Seller Docs · ¶8E / L(5)
Don't approve disclosures → cancel
🏡 Pro Move

On Day 1, walk your buyers through all 5 contingencies in plain language. Tell them exactly what each one protects and what it means to remove it. An educated buyer makes better decisions under pressure — and they'll trust you more when things get stressful around Day 15.

⚖️ Stay Within Your License

You can explain what contingencies are and how they work. You can advise your buyer on their options. You cannot give legal advice on what to do if there's a title dispute, tax lien interpretation, or contract breach. For those situations, refer your buyer to a qualified California real estate attorney. Your job is to protect them within the contract — their attorney handles what's beyond it.
