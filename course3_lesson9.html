Deposit Disputes

Liquidated Damages, Mutual Release, and the $1,000 Civil Penalty
~30 min readRPA ¶14H, ¶29, Civil Code §1057.35 Quiz Questions
💰 The Hardest Conversation

A colleague calls you. Their seller\'s deal fell apart after the buyer failed to close. The buyer walked. The seller sent a valid DCE, the buyer didn\'t close, and now both parties are fighting over the $37,500 deposit sitting in escrow. The buyer wants it back. The seller wants to keep it.

Who's right? It depends on a few critical factors — and this lesson explains exactly how to sort it out.

How Deposit Disputes Work

When a transaction cancels, the disposition of the earnest money deposit depends entirely on why the deal fell apart and how the cancellation was executed. There is no automatic rule that says "seller keeps the deposit" — it depends on the path taken.

The Mutual Release Requirement

Under ¶14H, when either party gives written notice of cancellation, both parties must sign mutual release instructions authorizing escrow to release the deposit. Escrow cannot release the deposit unilaterally — both signatures are required.

This creates the standoff: if the buyer won\'t sign a release to the seller and the seller won\'t sign a release to the buyer, the deposit sits in escrow. Neither party can touch it without the other's cooperation — or a court order or arbitration award.

🚨 The $1,000 Penalty — Civil Code §1057.3

Here's the leverage clause: California Civil Code §1057.3 states that if either party unreasonably refuses to sign cancellation instructions when they have no good faith dispute about who is entitled to the deposit, that party may be subject to a civil penalty of up to $1,000, plus attorney's fees and actual damages.

When Can the Seller Keep the Deposit?

The seller\'s right to retain the deposit as liquidated damages depends on three conditions under ¶29:

  1. The liquidated damages clause (¶29) was initialed by both buyer and seller at the time of the offer
  2. The buyer\'s deposit was 3% or less of the purchase price (for 1–4 unit residential property)
  3. The buyer breached the contract — meaning they failed to perform without a valid contractual right to cancel
📌 The Key Distinction: Buyer's RIGHT to Cancel vs. Breach

If the buyer canceled using a valid contingency (inspection, loan, etc.) — that is a RIGHT, not a breach. The seller gets the deposit BACK. Only if the buyer walks away with NO valid contractual right to cancel is their departure a breach — and only then does the liquidated damages clause apply.

Cancellation Path and Deposit Outcome

How Deal EndedWho Gets Deposit
Buyer canceled using valid contingency (inspection, loan, etc.)Buyer gets deposit back
Seller sent NBP, buyer didn\'t perform, seller canceledBuyer gets deposit back (minus fees)
Seller sent DCE, buyer didn\'t close, seller canceled + liquidated damages initialed + deposit ≤3%Seller may keep deposit
Seller canceled improperly (no NBP, no DCE)Buyer gets deposit back + potential legal liability for seller
Buyer walked without any valid right to cancelSeller may keep deposit (if liquidated damages initialed)

Practical Advice for Your Sellers

When a deposit dispute arises, advise your sellers to:

  • Never tell the buyer "we're keeping your deposit" unless all three ¶29 conditions are met
  • Sign the mutual cancellation instructions promptly when they agree the deposit goes to the buyer — refusing when there's no good faith dispute triggers the §1057.3 penalty
  • Consult a real estate attorney before refusing to sign any release in a contested deposit situation
✓ The Cardinal Rule

Neither agents nor escrow officers are qualified to advise which party is entitled to a disputed deposit. When there is a genuine dispute, refer both parties to an attorney.
