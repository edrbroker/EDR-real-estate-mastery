Seller Cancellation Rights

What You Can Cancel For, What You Cannot, and What Happens If You Get It Wrong
~30 min readRPA ¶14C, ¶14F5 Quiz Questions
📱 The Call You Dread

Day 14. David Chen calls you: "We got another offer today. It's $50,000 higher. Can we get out of this deal?"

The short answer is no. The longer answer requires you to understand exactly what seller cancellation rights look like — and what the consequences of improper cancellation are.

The Seller's Legitimate Cancellation Rights

Sellers have limited rights to cancel a transaction — they are not symmetric with buyer rights. Here's when a seller CAN legitimately cancel:

  • After delivering a valid NBP and the buyer fails to perform within 2 days
  • After delivering a valid DCE and the buyer fails to close within 3 days
  • If the seller has a valid seller contingency (not common in standard residential sales)
  • If the buyer commits a material breach of contract (e.g., fraud, misrepresentation)

What the Seller CANNOT Do

This is what agents need to communicate clearly to their seller clients:

  • Cannot cancel to accept a better offer — once in contract, the seller is bound
  • Cannot cancel because they changed their mind — seller's remorse is not a legal basis
  • Cannot cancel because a buyer contingency is still active — active contingencies protect the buyer, not the seller
  • Cannot issue an ultimatum without the proper notice form (NBP/DCE)
🚨 Wrongful Cancellation Consequences

If a seller improperly cancels — attempting to back out without a valid NBP, DCE, or contractual right — the buyer can: (1) seek specific performance (force the sale), (2) sue for damages, or (3) record a lis pendens on the property, preventing any sale to anyone else until litigation resolves.

Effect of Removal of Seller Contingencies

Under ¶14F(2), if the seller removes any contingency or cancellation rights, the seller is conclusively deemed to have: (i) satisfied themselves regarding that contingency, (ii) elected to proceed with the transaction, and (iii) given up any right to cancel based on that contingency. Removal is permanent — it cannot be undone.

The Disclosure Obligation Survives Cancellation

Even if the transaction cancels, the seller's obligation to disclose known material defects does not disappear. If the seller cancels and then relists — they must still disclose everything they know about the property. Any defect they became aware of during the cancelled transaction must be disclosed to the next buyer.

📌 Advising David Chen

You explain to David that they are contractually bound. Taking the higher offer isn't an option — unless the Okonkwos voluntarily agree to cancel, which is unlikely. The only path forward is to honor the contract and close the transaction. The right time to negotiate for a higher price was before signing.
