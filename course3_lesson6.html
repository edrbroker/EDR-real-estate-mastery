Notice to Seller to Perform (NSP)

When the Buyer Sends YOU a Notice — How to Respond Correctly
~30 min readRPA ¶14D, ¶14E5 Quiz Questions
📩 Day 10 — A Notice Arrives

You check your email. There is a signed Notice to Seller to Perform (NSP) from the Okonkwos' agent. The buyers are claiming the Chens have not yet delivered the HOA documents. The NSP gives the Chens 2 days to deliver.

David calls you, confused: "They're threatening us? Can they do that?" Yes, they can. And here's how you handle it.

What Is the NSP?

The Notice to Seller to Perform (C.A.R. Form NSP) is the buyer's equivalent of the seller's NBP. It is a formal notice that the buyer sends when the seller has failed to perform a contractual obligation. Just like the NBP, it is a required prerequisite before the buyer can cancel based on a seller default.

RPA Reference: ¶14D — Buyer's right to cancel for seller non-performance, which requires delivery of an NSP first. The NSP follows the exact same rules as the NBP under ¶14E.

When Can a Buyer Send an NSP?

The buyer may send an NSP when the seller fails to:

  • Deliver any item specified in ¶3N(1) by the deadline — TDS, SPQ, HOA documents, etc.
  • Perform any seller contractual obligation included in the agreement by the time specified
  • Remove seller contingencies (if any) by the deadline

Common triggers for NSPs include late disclosure delivery, failure to order or deliver HOA documents, and failure to make agreed repairs.

NSP Requirements — Same as the NBP

Under ¶14E, the NSP has the same three requirements as the NBP:

  1. Must be in writing
  2. Must be signed by the buyer
  3. Must give the seller at least 2 days after delivery to perform

And the same restrictions apply — it cannot be delivered more than 2 days before the scheduled performance day, and an invalid NSP is void.

How to Respond to an NSP

When your seller receives an NSP, your response strategy is straightforward:

  1. Identify exactly what the buyer is claiming you haven't done
  2. Verify whether the claim is valid — did you actually miss the obligation?
  3. Cure the defect immediately if valid — deliver the missing document within the 2-day window
  4. Dispute the NSP in writing if invalid — document that you already complied
✓ The Chens' Response

You pull up your delivery confirmation. The HOA documents were emailed to the buyer's agent on Day 8. You send a written response with the timestamp showing delivery. The NSP is based on a misunderstanding. Transaction continues.

📌 When the NSP Is Valid

If the seller genuinely missed a deadline, cure it within the 2-day window without dispute. Arguing over a valid NSP wastes the window and can cause the buyer to cancel with full deposit return.
