Notice to Buyer to Perform (NBP)

Your First Move When the Buyer Goes Silent — How to Use It Correctly
~30 min readRPA ¶14C, ¶14E5 Quiz Questions
⚠️ Scenario Shift — A Different Transaction

Let's step away from the Chens for a moment. Your colleague has a seller whose buyer has gone silent. Day 18. No contingency removal. No communication from the buyer's agent for 3 days. The seller is frustrated: "Can we just cancel? I have another offer waiting."

Not without the NBP. Here's how it works.

What Is the NBP?

The Notice to Buyer to Perform (C.A.R. Form NBP) is a formal written notice that the seller sends when the buyer has failed to meet a contractual deadline. It is not optional — it is a required prerequisite before the seller can cancel the transaction. Without a properly delivered NBP, any attempted cancellation by the seller is improper.

RPA Reference: ¶14C(1) — Seller may cancel if buyer does not deliver contingency removal by the deadline, but ONLY after first delivering the NBP.

When Can the Seller Send an NBP?

The seller may send an NBP for any of the following buyer failures:

  • Failure to remove contingencies by the deadline (¶14C(1))
  • Failure to deposit the initial or increased EMD (¶14C(2)(i))
  • Failure to deliver updated lender contact information (¶14C(2)(ii))
  • Failure to deliver FHA/VA cost/terms notice if applicable (¶14C(2)(iii))
  • Any other buyer contractual obligation not met by its deadline
📌 The Earliest You Can Send an NBP

Per ¶14E, the NBP may NOT be delivered earlier than 2 days before the scheduled performance day. You cannot send it early "just in case." Timing matters — an incorrectly timed NBP is invalid and void.

NBP Requirements — All Three Must Be Met

Under ¶14E, the NBP is valid only if it:

  1. Is in writing (C.A.R. Form NBP)
  2. Is signed by the seller (not the agent — the actual seller)
  3. Gives the buyer at least 2 days after delivery to perform
🚨 Common Mistake

Agents sometimes sign the NBP themselves or fail to allow the full 2-day window. Either error makes the NBP "deemed invalid and void" under ¶14E. If your NBP is void, you cannot cancel — you have to start over.

What Happens After the NBP?

The buyer has at least 2 days after the NBP is delivered to perform the required action. If they do — remove contingencies, deposit the EMD, etc. — the transaction continues. If they do NOT perform within the 2-day window, the seller may then formally cancel by delivering a written cancellation notice.

Critical point: After a valid NBP + non-performance, the seller's cancellation results in the return of the buyer's deposit (minus fees). The seller cannot keep the deposit simply because the buyer failed to remove contingencies. If the seller wants the deposit, that requires a different path — the DCE and liquidated damages clause. (More in Lessons 8 and 9.)

✓ What the NBP Actually Does

Think of the NBP as a formal warning shot. It puts the buyer on notice that they are in default and gives them one last chance to perform. It also creates the paper trail you need to justify a cancellation if they don't.
