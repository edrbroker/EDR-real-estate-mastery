When the Seller Pushes Back

Understanding the Notice to Buyer to Perform (NBP) and How to Respond
~30 min readRPA ¶14C, ¶14E5 Quiz Questions
📩 Day 19 — A Notice Arrives

It's Day 19. Your buyers still haven't removed contingencies. You've been working on getting the appraisal finalized. Then your email lights up: an NBP from the listing agent.

"Notice to Buyer to Perform." Two days to respond.

You know exactly what to do. Here's why.

What Is the NBP?

The Notice to Buyer to Perform (C.A.R. Form NBP) is a formal notice from the seller that the buyer has failed to meet a contractual deadline — most commonly, failure to remove contingencies by Day 17. It is a REQUIRED prerequisite before the seller can cancel the transaction.

RPA Reference: ¶14C(1) — The seller may only cancel due to buyer non-performance AFTER first delivering a valid NBP. Canceling without an NBP first is an improper cancellation.

NBP Requirements — All Three Must Be Met

Per ¶14E, an NBP is only valid if it:

  1. Is in writing (C.A.R. Form NBP)
  2. Is signed by the seller (not the listing agent — the actual seller)
  3. Gives the buyer at least 2 days after delivery to perform
🚨 Invalid NBP = No Cancellation

An NBP that doesn't meet all three requirements is "deemed invalid and void" per ¶14E. If the seller tries to cancel based on an invalid NBP, the cancellation itself is improper.

How to Respond to an NBP

Your buyer has at least 2 days from delivery to perform. Their options are:

  • Remove the contingencies (using the CR form) — transaction continues
  • Formally cancel (using the mutual cancellation instructions) — deposit is returned to buyer
  • Do nothing — after 2 days, seller may cancel, deposit returns to buyer
✓ The Okonkwo Situation

The appraisal came in at value the same day the NBP arrived. You get the CR signed that afternoon, removing the appraisal contingency. You still have the loan contingency active until Day 21. The NBP is resolved — transaction continues.
