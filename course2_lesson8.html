Close of Escrow Is Here —
But the Buyer Is Not Ready

The Demand to Close Escrow (DCE) Explained

~30 min RPA Paragraph 14G / C.A.R. Form DCE 5 Questions
📖 THE STORY CONTINUES

It is Day 29. Close of escrow is scheduled for tomorrow — Day 30. All contingencies have been removed. Appraisal came in at value. Loan approved. Inspection resolved. Insurance confirmed. Title clear.

Then at 4:30 PM the lender calls. 'We are running 3 days behind on funding. Loan docs will not be ready until Day 33.'

You hang up. The seller is expecting to close tomorrow. You call the listing agent to explain — and they say: 'My seller is sending a Demand to Close Escrow.'

This is the form most agents confuse with the NBP. The difference could cost your buyer their deposit.

What Is the Demand to Close Escrow?

The Demand to Close Escrow (C.A.R. Form DCE) is a formal written notice used when one party has failed to close escrow by the scheduled date. It gives the other party at least 3 days after delivery to close — and if they still do not close, the demanding party can cancel.

RPA Paragraph 14G — Demand to Close Escrow

Before either party may cancel for failure to close escrow, they must first deliver a DCE. The DCE shall: (i) be signed by the applicable buyer or seller; and (ii) give the other party at least 3 days after delivery to close escrow. A DCE may not be delivered any earlier than 3 days prior to the scheduled close of escrow date.

The Deposit Difference — Why This Matters

RPA Paragraph 14G — Deposit After DCE Cancellation

When a party cancels after a DCE, the RPA does NOT automatically require return of the deposit. The deposit is disputed — release requires either mutual signed instructions or a legal proceeding. The seller may be entitled to the deposit as liquidated damages (up to 3% of purchase price) if Paragraph 29 was properly initialed and the buyer failed to close without a valid reason.

The 3-Day Timing Rule

A common mistake: the seller waits until Day 30, realizes the buyer is not closing, and immediately sends a DCE. Now they must wait 3 more days — until Day 33 — before they can cancel. If the seller had sent the DCE on Day 27 (3 days before the scheduled COE), they could have cancelled as early as Day 30.

What to Do When Your Buyer Receives a DCE

1
Confirm all contingencies are removed

If any contingency is still active, a DCE may not be the correct form — the seller should have used an NBP.

2
Contact the lender immediately

Can the loan fund within 3 days? Push hard. Get a firm date in writing and communicate it to the seller's agent.

3
Request a written extension from the seller

If the delay is legitimate, many sellers will agree to a short extension rather than start over with a new buyer. Get it in writing.

4
If closing cannot happen in 3 days — consult an attorney

The deposit is at risk. This requires legal guidance, not just agent advice.

Prevent the DCE — Act Before It Arrives

If your buyer's lender signals a delay, request a written COE extension from the seller immediately — before the seller has grounds to send a DCE. A signed written extension is always better than a DCE situation. Proactive communication saves deposits.

Stay Within Your License

You can explain what a DCE is, help your buyer understand their options, push the lender, and negotiate a written extension. You cannot tell your buyer whether the seller will win a deposit dispute. If a DCE leads to cancellation and the deposit is contested, refer your buyer to a California real estate attorney immediately. Deposit disputes fall outside your license scope.
