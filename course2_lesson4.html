Contingency Removal

The Point of No Return — What It Means When Your Buyer Signs the CR

📋 Day 17 — The Big Decision

Day 17. You're on the phone with the Okonkwos. The inspection found a minor plumbing issue — the seller agreed to a $1,200 credit. The appraisal came in at value. The title is clean. The loan is conditionally approved.

"Should we remove contingencies?" Priya asks. This is the moment. You walk them through exactly what it means.

What Removing Contingencies Really Means

Signing the Contingency Removal (CR) form is one of the most consequential acts in a real estate transaction. Under ¶14F of the RPA, when a buyer removes a contingency, they are conclusively deemed to have: satisfied themselves regarding that contingency, elected to proceed with the transaction, and given up any right to cancel based on that contingency.

🚨 This Cannot Be Undone

Once a contingency is removed, it is gone permanently. There is no "I changed my mind" clause. If the buyer backs out after removing all contingencies without a valid remaining reason, their deposit is at risk.

Partial vs. Full Removal

Buyers can remove contingencies in stages. A buyer might remove the inspection and appraisal contingencies on Day 17 while keeping the loan contingency until Day 21. The CR form has checkboxes for each contingency, so you can remove some while keeping others active.

📌 Strategy Tip

In competitive markets, sellers often want buyers to remove all contingencies by Day 17. Discuss this with your clients before accepting the offer. Agreeing to shorten contingency periods in the offer is different from being pressured to remove them early during escrow.

After Contingency Removal — The Deposit Is At Risk

Once all contingencies are removed and the buyer has no other valid cancellation right, if they cancel without cause their deposit is at risk under the liquidated damages clause (¶29) — assuming it was initialed and the deposit does not exceed 3% of the purchase price.

RPA Reference: ¶14F — Effect of Removal of Contingencies. ¶29 — Liquidated Damages. The deposit becomes the seller's liquidated damages when a buyer breaches after removing all contingencies.
