The 17-Day Window

What Your Buyer Must Do Before It Closes — And What Happens If They Miss It

~30 min read RPA Paragraph 3L / 14B 5 Quiz Questions

Day 5. You've confirmed the EMD hit escrow. Marcus texted to say the lender called — they need a few more documents. Priya got three insurance quotes and they're all high because of the fire zone.

You look at your calendar. Day 17 is in 12 days. That's when every major contingency deadline lands. Twelve days to complete inspections, get the appraisal back, solve the insurance problem, review the title report, and review seller documents.

Twelve days sounds like a lot. But in a real transaction, it disappears fast.

Why 17 Days Is the Critical Number

Under the RPA (Revised 12/25), Paragraph 3L, the default time to remove all major buyer contingencies is 17 calendar days after acceptance. This single deadline covers the loan, appraisal, inspection, insurance, title, and seller document review contingencies simultaneously.

The parties can negotiate a different timeline — shorter in competitive markets, longer in complex transactions — but 17 days is what the RPA defaults to if nothing else is agreed upon.

⚠️ RPA Rule — Paragraph 14

Time periods in the RPA may only be extended, altered, or changed by mutual written agreement. One side cannot unilaterally extend a deadline. Both buyer and seller must agree in writing — and that agreement must be delivered to both parties before the deadline passes.

Day by Day: What Must Get Done

Day 0
Acceptance — clock starts
Send congratulations, build deal calendar, confirm EMD wire initiated. ¶1 / 3C
Day 1–3 (bus.)
EMD delivered to escrow
3 business days — the only business day deadline in the RPA. Confirm receipt with escrow. ¶3D(1)
Day 3
Loan application & verification of funds submitted
Buyer submits full loan application. Down payment and closing cost verification attached or due within 3 days. ¶H(2,3)
Day 5
Sign and return escrow instructions
5 days after delivery from escrow. Both parties sign. ¶N(2)
Day 7
Seller delivers all disclosures
TDS, SPQ, NHD, and all required docs. If late, buyer's review window extends to 5 days after delivery. ¶14A / N(1)
Day 7
HOA documents ordered (if applicable)
Seller pays the fee and orders HOA docs within 3 days of acceptance. ¶N(3)
Day 7–10
Schedule and complete home inspection
Buyer hires and pays. General, pest, roof, and any specialized inspections all before Day 17. ¶8C
Day 7–10
Review preliminary title report
Look for liens, easements, encumbrances. Flag anything unusual immediately. ¶8F / L(6)
Day 1–14
Start insurance search immediately
California's insurance market — especially fire zones — requires early action. Do not wait. ¶8D
Day 14
Appraisal ordered by lender
Lender typically orders by Day 5–7; results often come back around Day 14. Review immediately. ¶8B / L(2)
⚠️ Day 17
ALL MAJOR CONTINGENCY DEADLINES
Loan · Appraisal · Inspection · Insurance · Title · Seller Documents. Remove or cancel — in writing. ¶3L(1–6)

What Happens When Day 17 Passes Without Action?

This is where many new agents make a dangerous mistake. They assume that if the buyer doesn't remove a contingency by Day 17, the contingency just "stays active." That is not entirely correct.

Under RPA Paragraph 14B(4), even after Day 17 passes, the buyer retains the right to remove remaining contingencies or cancel — BUT only until the seller delivers a Notice to Buyer to Perform (NBP). Once the seller sends that notice, the buyer has a limited window to act. If they don't — the seller can cancel.

And critically: the seller cannot simply cancel without first sending the NBP. The process must be followed in the correct order. We cover NBP in detail in Lesson 5.

🚨 The Real Danger of Silence

If Day 17 passes and your buyer hasn't removed contingencies OR cancelled — they are in a gray zone. The deal is technically in breach. The seller can start the NBP process. Your buyer's deposit may be at risk depending on what happens next. Never let Day 17 pass silently. Either remove contingencies, cancel, or have a written extension in place.

How to Request a Written Extension

Sometimes 17 days isn't enough. The appraisal is delayed. The inspection found issues you're still evaluating. The lender needs more time. That's OK — but the solution must be documented properly.

To extend any deadline in the RPA, both buyer and seller must sign a written agreement — typically through an Amendment (C.A.R. Form AM) or a written addendum. This must be completed before the deadline passes.

As the buyer's agent, if you think you need more time, reach out to the listing agent early — not on Day 16 at 11pm. Professional communication and planning will get you an extension far more often than last-minute panicking.

🏡 Pro Move — The Day 10 Check-In

On Day 10, proactively contact your buyer, lender, and escrow officer. Confirm what's been done and what's still pending. You have 7 days to solve any problems before the deadline. At Day 16, your options are much more limited. The agents who never have emergencies are the ones who check in at Day 10.

⚖️ Stay Within Your License

If your buyer is considering missing the deadline on purpose as a negotiation tactic — advise them strongly against it. That is not within your scope of advice and could create legal exposure for you and your client. Any strategy involving intentional contract breach should go to their real estate attorney, not their agent.
