The 17-Day Window

How the Contingency Period Works and What You Must Do Before It Closes
~30 min readRPA ¶3L, ¶85 Quiz Questions
📋 Day 10 — The Race Is On

It's Day 10. The inspector has been out. The appraisal is scheduled for Day 12. You're waiting on the preliminary title report. Marcus texts: "Are we on track?"

You check your calendar. Day 17 is seven days away. Contingency removal deadline. Everything needs to come together.

The 17-Day Window in Practice

Days 1 through 17 are your buyer's protected window. During this time, they can cancel for any inspection, appraisal, title, or seller documents reason and get their full deposit back. Your job is to make sure every investigation is completed and every report is reviewed before Day 17.

📌 What Must Happen Before Day 17
  • Home inspection completed and reviewed
  • Appraisal ordered and returned (or contingency removal requested)
  • Preliminary title report reviewed
  • All seller disclosures reviewed (TDS, SPQ, NHD)
  • HOA documents reviewed if applicable

The Contingency Removal Form (CR)

When your buyer is satisfied and ready to move forward, they sign the Contingency Removal form (C.A.R. Form CR). This form removes some or all contingencies. It can be done in stages — your buyer can remove inspection before removing the loan contingency, for example.

RPA Reference: ¶8 — Buyer Investigations. ¶14F — Effect of Removal of Contingencies. Once a contingency is removed, the buyer conclusively agrees to: (i) satisfied themselves, (ii) elected to proceed, and (iii) given up the right to cancel for that reason.

What Happens If Day 17 Passes Without Removal?

If your buyer has NOT removed contingencies by Day 17, the seller can send a Notice to Buyer to Perform (NBP). This gives the buyer 2 days to either remove or formally cancel. If the buyer does neither, the seller can cancel and — importantly — the buyer's deposit is RETURNED (not forfeited) in this scenario.

🚨 Don't Miss the Deadline

Even if your buyer wants to remove contingencies, THEY MUST DO IT IN WRITING using the CR form. A verbal "we're good to go" is not contingency removal under California law.
