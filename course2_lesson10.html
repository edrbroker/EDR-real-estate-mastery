Cancellation Rights — The Full Picture

Under the RPA, the buyer has three scenarios where they have the right to cancel and get their deposit back — all tied directly to the contingency and cancellation framework.

RPA Paragraph 14D — Buyer's Three Cancellation Rights

(1) Seller failed to remove a seller contingency by the deadline — buyer sends NSP — can cancel and recover deposit

(2) Seller failed to deliver required documents or perform a contractual obligation — buyer sends NSP — can cancel and recover deposit

(3) Buyer exercises any active buyer contingency in good faith — as long as that contingency has not already been removed in writing

C.A.R. Form CC — Cancellation of Contract

When a buyer decides to cancel, the proper form is C.A.R. Form CC (Cancellation of Contract, Release of Deposit and Cancellation of Escrow). Cancellation must be in writing using the correct form and delivered to the seller.

Form Name
C.A.R. Form CC — Cancellation of Contract
Signed By
Buyer — seller must sign mutual release for deposit to be returned
Deposit Release
Both parties must sign mutual instructions for escrow to release funds
Verbal Cancellation
Has no legal effect — must always be in writing

When Is the Deposit Protected?

✓
Buyer cancels based on an active contingency in good faith

Loan denied, appraisal too low, inspection unacceptable, cannot get insurance, title issue — while the contingency is still active. Deposit is returned.

✓
Seller cancels after issuing an NBP

RPA requires seller to return buyer's deposit (less fees) when canceling after an NBP. Seller cannot keep it.

✓
Buyer cancels after sending NSP for seller non-performance

If the seller missed their obligations and buyer properly issued an NSP before cancelling, the deposit is returned.

✓
Mutual agreement to cancel

Both parties agree to cancel and sign mutual release instructions. Deposit returned as agreed.

When Is the Deposit at Risk?

Situations Where the Buyer May Lose Their Deposit

(1) Buyer cancels after all contingencies are removed — without a valid contractual reason. Seller may claim liquidated damages up to 3% of purchase price if Paragraph 29 was initialed.

(2) Seller cancels after properly issuing a DCE and the buyer failed to close with no valid excuse — deposit is not automatically returned.

(3) Buyer refuses to sign mutual release instructions — escrow holds funds until legal resolution. Civil penalty of up to $1,000 may apply for bad faith refusal. (Civil Code Section 1057.3)

(4) Buyer cancels in bad faith — claiming a contingency as a pretext when the real reason is something else.

The Liquidated Damages Cap — Paragraph 29

If Paragraph 29 was initialed by both parties, the maximum the seller can claim as liquidated damages is 3% of the purchase price for 1 to 4 unit owner-occupied residential properties. For 5555 Winners Lane at $1,250,000: the maximum liquidated damages would be $37,500.

The Mutual Release Process

1
Buyer signs C.A.R. Form CC

Exercises the right to cancel based on a specific contractual right (active contingency, seller non-performance, etc.).

2
CC is delivered to the seller's agent

Delivery is the key. Confirm receipt in writing.

3
Seller signs the mutual release of deposit portion of the CC

This authorizes escrow to return the deposit. If the seller refuses to sign — the deposit is disputed.

4
Escrow receives signed mutual instructions and releases deposit

Funds returned to the buyer, less any fees already paid by escrow on the buyer's behalf.

What to Tell Marcus When He Asks About Walking Away

"Right now your contingencies are still active. If something in this deal is not working for you — the inspection, the loan, the insurance — this is the window to cancel and get your deposit back. Once you remove those contingencies, the equation changes. You are committed. So before we remove anything, let us make sure you are truly satisfied with every piece of this deal."

Stay Within Your License

You can walk your buyers through the cancellation process and help them complete C.A.R. Form CC. You cannot tell them whether they will definitely get their deposit back in a contested situation, advise them on legal strategy in a deposit dispute, or represent them in arbitration. If a seller refuses to sign the mutual release and the deposit is contested, refer your buyers to a California real estate attorney immediately.
