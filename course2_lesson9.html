NBP vs. DCE:
The Form That Can Cost Your Buyer Their Deposit

Side-by-Side Comparison — Know the Difference Cold

~30 min RPA Paragraph 14C / 14E / 14G 5 Questions
📖 THE STORY CONTINUES

You are mentoring a newer agent on your team. She just got her first listing and a buyer deal in the same week. She calls you in a panic.

'I have two situations and I do not know which form to use. On my listing, the buyer has not removed contingencies and the deadline was yesterday. On my buyer deal, my buyer cannot close on time — the lender is 5 days behind.'

You take a breath. This is exactly where agents make the most costly mistakes. Wrong form. Wrong timing. Wrong deposit outcome.

The Most Common Agent Mistake in California

Every year, California agents use the wrong cancellation form in the wrong situation. The most frequent error: using an NBP when a DCE is required, or vice versa. The consequences can include an invalid cancellation attempt — and in DCE situations, a buyer keeping their deposit when the seller thought they had a claim to it.

The Decision Tree — Which Form?

Ask These Questions in Order

Step 1: Are there still open contingencies?
Yes → Use NBP (or NSP if buyer is acting against seller)
No → Go to Step 2

Step 2: Has the scheduled close of escrow date passed (or is it within 3 days) and the party has not closed?
Yes → Use DCE
No → Likely still an NBP situation

Real Scenarios — Which Form?

Scenario A — Day 18. Buyer has not removed loan contingency. Deadline was Day 17.

Answer: NBP. The contingency is open. Use NBP, not DCE.

Scenario B — Day 31. All contingencies removed Day 18. Scheduled close Day 30. Buyer's lender did not fund.

Answer: DCE. All contingencies are gone. The only issue is failure to close. Use DCE, give 3 days.

Scenario C — Day 27. All contingencies removed. Scheduled close Day 30. Seller suspects buyer will not fund in time.

Answer: DCE. Day 27 is exactly 3 days before COE — the earliest allowed. If the buyer closes Day 30, the DCE is moot. If not, the seller can cancel after Day 30.

The Deposit Outcome — Memorize This

After NBP Cancellation
Seller MUST return buyer's deposit (less fees). Seller cannot keep it.
After DCE Cancellation
Deposit NOT automatically returned. Seller may have a claim. Requires mutual instructions or legal action to release.
The One Sentence That Saves Deals

"Are all contingencies removed?" If yes and closing has not happened — DCE. If no — NBP. Burn that into your brain. It will save your clients' deposits and save you from E&O claims.

Stay Within Your License

You can identify the correct form to use and explain its implications to your client. You cannot determine who is legally entitled to the deposit in a contested dispute — that is a legal question. If a cancellation results in a deposit dispute, refer your client to a California real estate attorney immediately. Never advise your client they will definitely win or lose a deposit battle.
