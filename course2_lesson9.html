NBP vs. DCE

Know the Difference — Which Tool to Use and When
~30 min readRPA ¶14C, ¶14G5 Quiz Questions
📋 The Confusion Zone

A fellow agent calls you in a panic. "The seller missed the repair deadline. Do I send an NBP or a DCE?" This is one of the most common mix-ups in California real estate. Let's sort it out once and for all.

NBP vs. DCE — The Core Difference

FeatureNBPDCE
Full NameNotice to Buyer to PerformDemand to Close Escrow
Sent BySeller (to buyer)Either party
Used WhenBuyer missed contingency or contractual deadlineOther party failed to close escrow by COE date
Minimum Notice2 days3 days
Earliest Delivery2 days before scheduled performance day3 days before scheduled COE date
After Non-PerformanceBuyer deposit returned to buyerMay be entitled to liquidated damages (seller path)

Quick Decision Guide

Ask yourself: What specifically did the other party fail to do?

  • Failed to remove contingencies → NBP (if you are seller) or NSP (if you are buyer for seller failures)
  • Failed to deposit EMD → NBP
  • Failed to close escrow by the COE date → DCE
  • Seller failed to deliver disclosures → NSP (buyer sends to seller)
📌 The Cardinal Rule

NBP/NSP = contractual obligation failures. DCE = failure to close on time. Never use a DCE for a contingency issue, and never use an NBP for a closing date issue.
