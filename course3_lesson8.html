Demand to Close Escrow (DCE)

When the Buyer Won't Close — Your Path to Enforce or Walk Away
~30 min readRPA ¶14G5 Quiz Questions
⏰ Day 22 — Closing Day Passes

The scheduled close of escrow was today — June 23. Escrow didn't close. The buyer's lender is requesting one more day to fund. The Chens are furious: "We already gave them an extension. Can we cancel now and keep the deposit?"

Not yet. But the DCE is the tool you use to get there. Here's exactly how it works.

What Is the DCE?

The Demand to Close Escrow (C.A.R. Form DCE) is a formal notice that either party sends when the other has failed to close escrow by the scheduled date. For sellers, it is the mechanism to demand that the buyer fund and close — or face cancellation. Unlike the NBP (which handles contingency issues), the DCE specifically addresses failure to close escrow.

RPA Reference: ¶14G — Before either party can cancel for failure to close escrow, the canceling party must first deliver a DCE. The DCE gives the other party at least 3 days to close.

DCE Requirements Under ¶14G

The DCE has specific requirements that mirror the NBP/NSP framework:

  1. Must be in writing (C.A.R. Form DCE)
  2. Must be signed by the applicable buyer or seller
  3. Must give the other party at least 3 days after delivery to close escrow
  4. Cannot be delivered earlier than 3 days before the scheduled close of escrow date
📌 DCE vs. NBP — The Key Timing Difference

NBP requires 2-day minimum notice. DCE requires 3-day minimum notice. This is not a typo — the DCE has a longer minimum window. Always double-check the form and count your days carefully.

When to Use DCE vs. NBP

SituationCorrect Tool
Buyer hasn't removed contingencies by deadlineNBP
Buyer hasn't deposited EMDNBP
Buyer hasn't closed by the scheduled close dateDCE
Buyer closed late — seller wants to demand final closeDCE
Seller hasn't delivered disclosuresNSP (from buyer)
🚨 Critical Rule

A DCE may not be delivered earlier than 3 days before the scheduled close of escrow date. If delivered too early, it is "deemed invalid and void" — just like an improperly timed NBP. Always check the COE date before sending.

What Happens After a DCE?

If the buyer closes escrow within the 3-day window — the transaction closes and everyone moves on. If they do NOT close within the 3-day window after DCE delivery, the seller may cancel. And here's the key difference from an NBP cancellation:

When a seller cancels after a valid DCE + buyer failure to close, the seller may be entitled to the buyer's deposit as liquidated damages under ¶29 — if the liquidated damages clause was initialed and certain conditions are met. This is the path to keeping the deposit. We go deeper on this in Lesson 9.

✓ The Chens' Situation

You wait one day, then deliver a DCE on June 24. The buyer's lender funds the loan on June 25. Escrow closes on June 26 — within the 3-day DCE window. The Chens get their money. No cancellation needed. The DCE served its purpose: it created urgency without destroying the deal.
