The HOA Contingency — Section L(7)

When a property is part of a condominium, planned development, or any common interest development, the buyer gets an additional contingency: the Common Interest Disclosures contingency (RPA Paragraph 8G / L(7)).

RPA Paragraph 8G / L(7) — Common Interest Disclosures Contingency

The agreement is contingent upon the buyer's review and approval of Common Interest Disclosures required by California Civil Code Section 4525. The deadline is 17 days after acceptance OR 5 days after delivery of HOA documents — whichever is LATER.

What the Seller Must Deliver

The seller must order HOA documents within 3 days of acceptance (RPA Paragraph N(3)) and the seller pays the fee. Civil Code Section 4525 specifies what must be provided:

CC&Rs
Rules governing what owners can and cannot do with the property
Bylaws
How the HOA is governed and operated
Financial Documents
HOA budget, reserve fund study, and financial statements
Meeting Minutes
Reveals pending assessments, litigation, and disputes
Rules and Regulations
Pet policies, rental restrictions, parking rules, renovation approval
Pending Assessments
Any special assessments coming — can mean thousands in extra costs

What Your Buyer Must Review

1
Reserve Fund Health

A well-funded HOA has 70% or more of projected needs in reserve. Underfunded = future special assessments. This is a major red flag.

2
Pending or Threatened Litigation

Check board minutes for lawsuits against or by the HOA. This can affect insurance, financing, and property value.

3
Rental Restrictions

Some HOAs prohibit renting units entirely. If your buyers ever want to rent the property, this is critical.

4
Special Assessments

Any approved but unbilled assessment the new buyer may be responsible for. Check the budget and minutes carefully.

5
Pet and Renovation Policies

Does the HOA allow your buyers' pets? Do they need board approval to renovate? CC&Rs spell this out.

HOA and Financing — The Hidden Risk

Non-Warrantable Condo Warning

Some lenders will not fund loans on properties in HOAs with too many non-owner-occupied units, HOAs in active litigation, or HOAs with less than 10% reserves — called a non-warrantable condo. If this applies, the Okonkwos' conventional loan may be denied. Confirm HOA warrantability with the lender BEFORE removing the loan contingency. This is a common deal killer for new agents.

HOA Prorations at Close of Escrow

Under RPA Paragraph 17, HOA regular assessments are prorated at close of escrow on a 30-day month basis. Special or emergency assessments approved or due before close are paid by the seller. Assessments that come after close are the buyer's responsibility. Always confirm the proration with escrow.

Pro Move on HOA Deals

On Day 1 of any HOA transaction, confirm the seller's agent has ordered the HOA documents. HOA document packages can take 7 to 10 days to arrive — a late order can push documents past your contingency deadline. If HOA documents are not ordered within 3 days, send an NSP immediately. Do not let the HOA doc timeline catch you off guard.

Stay Within Your License

You can help buyers understand what HOA documents to review and flag concerns. You cannot provide legal interpretations of CC&Rs, advise on HOA litigation exposure, or tell buyers whether an underfunded reserve is legally acceptable. For complex HOA document review, recommend a real estate attorney specializing in common interest developments.
